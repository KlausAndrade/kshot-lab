Heart

Beginner · the gateway pour
How to
Final result
  1. Fill phase (first ⅔): Hold jug high, pour from ~10 cm directly into centre. The stream punctures the crema and mixes underneath — no white surface yet.
  2. Float phase: When cup is ⅔ full, lower jug to ~1 cm above surface. White milk breaks through and forms a circle.
  3. Draw phase: Let the circle grow into a disk, then lift slightly and pull the stream straight through the middle toward the far side — splits the disk into a heart.

Rosetta

Intermediate · rhythm is everything
How to
Final result
  1. Fill high: Punch through the crema in the centre, filling to about ⅔. Crema and milk mix, no white yet.
  2. Drop low, start wiggling: Get the spout right on the surface at the far side of the cup. Start a side-to-side wrist wiggle — fast, small amplitude, metronomic. White "leaves" appear.
  3. Walk back: Slowly move your hand toward yourself while wiggling. Each new leaf pushes the previous ones outward, fanning them into the rosetta shape.
  4. Pull through: Lift the jug slightly and drag a thin line straight through the whole length. That's the stem that defines the rosetta.

Tulip

Intermediate · stack discipline
How to
Final result
  1. Fill phase: Same as heart — high pour through the centre, mix everything up until cup is ⅔ full.
  2. First blob (far side): Drop low at the far edge and let a round white blob form.
  3. Stop, move, restart: Briefly pause the pour, move the spout 1–2 cm toward yourself, restart — a new blob pushes the first one forward.
  4. Repeat 2–4 times: Each pulse stacks in front of the last. More pulses = more layered tulip.
  5. Pull through: Finish by lifting and dragging a thin line through all the layers to unify them.

Plain pour

Starter · the texture test
How to
Final result
  1. Pour from height straight into the centre, keeping the jug ~10 cm above the cup the entire time.
  2. Don't drop low. Never bring the spout close to the surface — no design is attempted on this pour.
  3. Read the result: A neat white circle forming at the end means your microfoam is good. If it disappears or bubbles surface, your texturing needs work before attempting art.

Milk texturing (the prerequisite)

Foundation · without this, no art
  1. Fill the jug ⅓ full of cold milk straight from the fridge. Cold milk = more time to texture before it overheats.
  2. Purge the wand first to clear condensation.
  3. Air phase (3–4 seconds): Position the tip just below the surface. You should hear a controlled "paper-tearing" sound — that's air being introduced. Too deep = no air; too shallow = screaming + big bubbles.
  4. Texture phase: Submerge the tip ~1 cm below surface, off-centre. The milk spins into a whirlpool that incorporates the air into micro-sized bubbles. Silence is good — roaring means you're still pulling air.
  5. Stop at ~60 °C. Jug becomes uncomfortably hot to hold. Over 70 °C kills the foam.
  6. Bang and swirl: Tap the jug hard on the counter to pop large bubbles, then swirl to polish. Milk should look like wet paint — glossy, seamless, moving as one.

Common failures

Diagnostic · when art doesn't appear
  1. White sinks immediately: Foam is too thin, no body. Steam longer / introduce more air at start.
  2. Pattern blurs as you pour: Milk is too thick, or crema is weak. Polish more, or dial in your espresso shot first.
  3. Big bubbles on surface: Air introduced too late or too aggressively. Bang the jug harder before pouring.
  4. Design wobbles / streaks: Inconsistent flow rate from the spout. Keep your wrist and forearm locked, move only the hand.
  5. Rosetta has no leaves: Wiggle too slow, or spout too far from surface.
  6. Heart isn't symmetrical: You dragged the line at an angle. Pull straight through the axis of the cup.
Daily

After every session

  1. Purge the steam wand for 1–2 seconds to clear any milk inside the tip.
  2. Wipe the steam wand immediately with a damp cloth while still warm — cold milk residue is nearly impossible to remove later.
  3. Knock out the puck, rinse the portafilter and basket under hot water.
  4. Wipe the group-head gasket and shower screen with a soft cloth or brush.
  5. Empty and rinse the drip tray and grinds bin if they're getting full.
  6. Wipe the machine exterior and the heated cup rest.
Why it matters: Milk residue inside the steam wand cooks into a hard deposit and can clog the tip. Wet coffee grounds left on the group-head gasket dry and crack the rubber over time.
Weekly

Group-head soak

  1. Insert the cleaning disc (rubber blanking disc) into the single-wall basket.
  2. Add a small amount of espresso machine cleaner (Cafiza or similar) on top of the disc.
  3. Lock the portafilter into the group head.
  4. Run a short manual shot — water pools against the disc and soaks the shower screen.
  5. Remove portafilter and rinse thoroughly. Run clean water through the group head a couple of times to flush residue.
  6. Remove the shower screen (if accessible) and soak separately in warm cleaner solution for 10 minutes, then reinstall.
Note: The Barista Pro doesn't have a 3-way solenoid valve, so traditional backflushing isn't possible. The blanking-disc soak is the equivalent routine.
Weekly

Steam wand deep clean

  1. Fill a small cup with warm water and a drop of steam wand cleaner.
  2. Submerge the steam tip and let it soak 10–15 minutes — dissolves hardened milk.
  3. Unscrew the steam tip (once the machine is cool) and clear each hole with the supplied pin or a paperclip.
  4. Rinse under hot water, reattach firmly, and purge with steam to clear the internal tube.
CLEAN ME light

Automatic clean cycle

  1. Drop a cleaning tablet into the single-wall basket with the rubber cleaning disc on top.
  2. Lock the portafilter into the group head.
  3. Make sure the drip tray is empty and the water tank is full.
  4. With the machine on, press and hold 1 CUP, 2 CUP, and POWER simultaneously.
  5. The machine runs an automatic 5-minute clean cycle — do not interrupt.
  6. When finished, remove the portafilter, discard the residue, and rinse everything thoroughly.
  7. Run a couple of blank shots through the group head to flush any remaining cleaner.
Triggered by: A fixed shot count (around 200 shots). Don't ignore the warning — cleaner buildup in the group affects taste.
DESCALE light

Descaling cycle

  1. Empty the drip tray and remove the water filter from the tank.
  2. Fill the water tank with descaling solution mixed per the product instructions (typically citric-acid or commercial descaler + water).
  3. With the machine off, press and hold the POWER button until the descale menu appears, then start the cycle.
  4. The machine alternates between flushing water through the group head and the steam/hot-water circuits. Keep a large vessel under each outlet.
  5. When prompted, empty the tank, refill with fresh water, and run the rinse cycle — this is critical to flush out any remaining descaler.
  6. Reinstall the water filter and wipe everything down.
Why: Limescale builds up on heating element and boiler walls, insulates the element, makes it work harder, reduces lifespan, and tastes awful. Frequency depends on water hardness — harder water = descale more often.
Monthly

Grinder hopper & burrs

  1. Grind through any remaining beans in the hopper until empty.
  2. Remove the hopper, wipe it clean of coffee oils with a dry cloth. Avoid water unless thoroughly dried — moisture in the grinder is bad.
  3. Run the machine with the empty hopper for a few seconds to eject any retained grounds.
  4. For a deeper clean every few months, grind a small handful of grinder cleaning tablets (Grindz or similar) through the burrs, followed by a few grams of coffee to purge residue.
  5. Brush out the grind chute with the supplied brush.
Retention reminder: Every time you change grind size, the first few grams are still at the old setting. Purge 2 g before a shot after adjustments — or accept that the first shot is a throwaway.
Every 3 months

Water filter replacement

  1. Soak the new filter cartridge in cold water for 5 minutes to activate.
  2. Rinse under running cold water for 5 seconds to flush loose carbon particles.
  3. Reset the date indicator on the filter holder to three months from today.
  4. Insert the filter firmly into its holder in the water tank.
  5. Refill the tank and run one blank water cycle through the group head to prime.
Why: The carbon filter reduces chlorine and some scale-causing minerals. Expired filters do neither, and chlorinated water dulls flavour significantly.
As needed

Drip tray & grinds bin

  1. Empty the drip tray when the red "EMPTY ME" indicator floats above the grille.
  2. Wash the drip tray and grille in warm soapy water. Rinse thoroughly.
  3. Empty the knock box / grinds container into compost or bin — wet grounds mould quickly.
  4. Dry completely before reinstalling — standing water invites bacteria and smells.
Every shot

Group flush (the one most people skip)

  1. Before locking the portafilter in, run a 1–2 second manual flush through the group head.
  2. This clears any old grounds clinging to the shower screen and stabilises the group temperature.
  3. Wipe the gasket area with a dry cloth.
  4. Now dose, distribute, tamp and lock in.
Why: Old grounds left on the shower screen introduce stale bitter flavours into a fresh shot. A two-second flush costs nothing and noticeably improves taste.
